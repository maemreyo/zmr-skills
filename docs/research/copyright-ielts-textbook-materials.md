## Risk Guidance Matrix

### 🟢 GREEN: Low Risk (generally permissible without permission)

| Activity | Legal Basis | Jurisdiction Limit | Key Condition |
|----------|-------------|-------------------|---------------|
| **Hand-copying** short extracts from the textbook onto a whiteboard/blackboard during a lesson | CDPA s.32(1)(b) – not by reprographic process; Vietnam Art.25(1)(a) – self-copy by hand | UK / Vietnam | Must be non-commercial; for instruction only |
| **Displaying** a short passage from the textbook on an interactive whiteboard to illustrate a point | CDPA s.32 (fair dealing for illustration); UK IPO guidance confirms this | UK | Must be solely to illustrate a point; non-commercial; sufficient acknowledgement |
| **Quoting** short extracts (<400 words) from the textbook in teacher-created materials with full citation | Cambridge permission policy – pre-authorised for <400 word prose extracts | Global (CUP books) | Must include full citation; CUP must hold the rights |

| **Creating original lesson plans** that reference the textbook structure (e.g., "see page 42") without reproducing text | Independent creation; not a derivative use; factual reference only | Global | No substantial copying of expression |
| **Recommending** students purchase/use the textbook alongside teacher-created materials | Complements the market; does not substitute | Global | N/A — this supports sales |

## Jurisdiction Notes

### United Kingdom (publishing jurisdiction & primary legal framework)

- Cambridge University Press is based in Cambridge, UK. Its publications are protected under UK copyright law (Copyright, Designs and Patents Act 1988).
- The UK has a **fair dealing** framework (not US-style fair use). Educational exceptions are enumerated in ss.32–36 CDPA.
- **Key limitation**: s.36(3) and s.36(6) — if a **licence is available** (e.g., CLA licence covering the work), the statutory exception does not apply. You must use the licence.
- CLA licence covers Cambridge University Press publications in the UK. Most state-funded schools hold this centrally.
- **International application**: UK law governs the CUP-IELTS textbooks themselves. But Vietnamese law governs how they are *used* in Vietnam (territoriality principle — see UK IPO guidance: "you need to ensure that you comply with the laws of the countries in which you provide online resources").

### Vietnam (user's jurisdiction)

- Vietnam's IP Law 2022 (07/2022/QH15, effective Jan 1, 2023) significantly expanded educational exceptions in Article 25.
- **Key provisions for teachers** (Art.25(1)):
  - (a) Self-copy ONE copy for personal study/research — NOT by copying device (hand-copying only)
  - (b) Reasonable copy of PART of a work by copying device for research/study — non-commercial
  - (c) Reasonable use as illustration in lectures — may include internal computer network with technical access controls
  - (e) Library use for non-commercial purposes
- **Three-step test** applied: Art.25(2) — use must not conflict with normal exploitation or unreasonably prejudice the author.
- **Derivative works**: Translation and reproduction for teaching/research of non-commercial nature by Vietnamese organizations is permitted under developing-country provisions (Art.26(4)), but requires application to the Ministry of Culture, Sports and Tourism (Decree 17/2023, Art.36-37) — a compulsory licensing process.
- **Uncertainty**: Vietnamese law uses terms like "reasonable" without bright-line percentages for partial copying by device. Decree 17/2023 Art.25 says "no more than one copy containing a part of the works" but does not define the proportion. Academic commentary (Phạm Hoàng Phúc, 2022) notes this uncertainty and recommends clearer quantification.

### International Framework (WIPO/Berne)

- Both the UK and Vietnam are signatories to the Berne Convention and TRIPS Agreement.
- **Berne three-step test** (Art.9(2)): exceptions must be (1) certain special cases, (2) not conflict with normal exploitation, (3) not unreasonably prejudice legitimate interests.
- **Berne Art.10(2)**: specifically permits use of works "by way of illustration in publications, broadcasts or sound or visual recordings for teaching, provided such utilization is compatible with fair practice" — this is the international anchoring provision for teaching exceptions.
- **WIPO Copyright Treaty (WCT) Art.10**: permits carrying forward and extending exceptions into the digital environment.
- **Important**: No international instrument authorizes the reproduction of entire tests/practice exams for teaching without permission. The three-step test's "normal exploitation" prong protects the market for test preparation materials.

## Key Distinctions

| Concept | Teacher-Created Supplementary Materials | Direct Reproduction of Textbook Content |
|---------|---------------------------------------|----------------------------------------|
| **Original authorship** | Teacher adds new expression, structure, insight | Copies or closely paraphrases the preexisting work |
| **Market substitution** | Complements the textbook; may enhance its value | May substitute for purchase of the textbook or official supplements |
| **Copyright status** | New, independent copyright in the teacher's original contribution | Derivative work; requires permission from original rights holder |
| **Fair dealing analysis** | Stronger case (transformative, limited, non-commercial) | Weaker case (non-transformative if the material is the same) |

### The "Derivative Work" Risk (Pearson v. Chegg Lesson)

The Pearson v. Chegg lawsuit (Northern District of California, filed 2021) illustrates the central risk: **when do independently created supplementary materials infringe the derivative-work right in the textbook?**

- Pearson's theory: Chegg's answer sets were "based upon, and necessarily derive from" Pearson's questions. Even where answers didn't quote the questions, they tracked the selection and arrangement.
- The case was settled (2023) before a ruling, so the law remains **unsettled** on this point.
- **Implication for IELTS materials**: Creating answer guides, model answers for Writing tasks, or scaffolding worksheets that closely track the textbook's practice tests may raise derivative-work concerns.

## Recommended Safeguards

1. **Always create original content** — write your own passages, questions, and explanations inspired by the textbook's *topics*, not derived from its *expression*.
2. **Never reproduce practice tests** — direct students to the textbook or official digital resources for test practice.
3. **Use the "inspired by, not copied from" principle** — if a student could use your materials as a substitute for buying the textbook, the use is likely too close.
4. **Attribute everything** — even where permission is not required, full citation has legal benefits (demonstrates good faith) and is required under most exceptions.
5. **Know your institution's licence** — if your school holds a CLA licence or equivalent (Vietnam's VRO), check what it covers.
6. **Keep materials within the secure network** (LMS, password-protected class page) — never on public websites.
7. **Limit copying to <5% of any single work** per year — the safest quantitative benchmark across both UK and CLA frameworks.
8. **Do not monetize** — any commercial sale of materials incorporating textbook content loses virtually all exception protection.
9. **The "IELTS" name and logos are trademarks** — do not use them on teacher-created materials without permission.
10. **When in doubt, seek permission** — Cambridge's permissions team (via PLSClear) processes requests; response times are ~3 weeks.

---

## What This Guidance Does NOT Cover

- Copyright in images, photographs, and third-party materials embedded within the textbook (Cambridge's copyright page warns that it may not hold rights to all illustrations — always check caption/source).
- Moral rights of authors (right of attribution, right against derogatory treatment).
- Data protection/privacy implications of sharing materials online.
- Contractual terms that may apply through institutional subscription agreements (e.g., Cambridge One, Cambridge GO).
- Specific laws of any jurisdiction other than the UK and Vietnam as they relate to use within those territories.

## ## CLAIMS

**Claim 1**: Cambridge University Press requires written permission to photocopy any publication unless it is clearly labelled as "photocopiable" or covered by a national reprographic rights organisation (RRO) licence.
- **Source**: Cambridge Permissions FAQs — "Except as provided under national law, written permission is required to photocopy any Cambridge publication unless it is clearly labelled as 'photocopiable'." [Cambridge Permissions FAQs](https://www.cambridge.org/rights-and-permissions/permissions-faqs)

**Claim 2**: For the Cambridge IELTS practice test series (IELTS 15, 17, and similar), only the sample answer sheets at the back of the book are designated as "Photocopiable." All other content requires written permission.
- **Source**: IELTS 15 Academic copyright notice: "It is normally necessary for written permission for copying to be obtained in advance from a publisher. The sample answer sheets at the back of this book are designed to be copied and distributed in class. ... Only those pages that carry the wording '© Cambridge Assessment 2020 Photocopiable' may be copied." [IELTS 15 copyright info](https://assets.cambridge.org/97811087/81619/copyright/9781108781619_copyright_info.pdf)

**Claim 3**: The IELTS test name and logos are registered trade marks owned jointly by the British Council, IDP IELTS, and Cambridge University Press & Assessment. Their use without permission is prohibited.
- **Source**: IELTS copyright and trademark statement — "IELTS, IELTS logos, and various other marks ... are registered trade marks or trade marks of the IELTS Partners ... You must not use, copy, reproduce, modify, distribute, republish, display or communicate these trade marks in any way." [IELTS copyright page](https://ielts.org/legal/ielts-copyright-and-trade-mark-statement)

**Claim 4**: Cambridge University Press grants permission freely for short prose extracts (less than 400 words), a single figure, or a single table without requiring a formal request, provided a full citation is included.
- **Source**: Cambridge Rights & Permissions — "Cambridge University Press grants permission freely for the reproduction in another work of a short prose extract (less than 400 words), a single figure or a single table in which it holds rights ... a request for permission need not be submitted, but the reproduced material must be accompanied by a full citation." [Cambridge permission page](https://www.cambridge.org/us/information/rights/permission.htm)

**Claim 5**: Under the UK Copyright, Designs and Patents Act 1988, s.36 (as amended 2014), an educational establishment may copy up to 5% of a work per 12 months for non-commercial instruction, provided a licence is not available. If a CLA licence is available, the licence terms apply instead.
- **Source**: CDPA 1988 s.36 — "Not more than 5% of a work may be copied under this section by or on behalf of an educational establishment in any period of 12 months" and "Acts which would otherwise be permitted by this section are not permitted if, or to the extent that, licences are available." [legislation.gov.uk](https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/1988/48/section/36)

**Claim 6**: The UK's fair dealing exception (s.32 CDPA) allows copying for illustration for instruction if it is non-commercial, done by a person giving or receiving instruction, and accompanied by sufficient acknowledgement. This applies to all types of work, including digital use.
- **Source**: CDPA 1988 s.32 — "Fair dealing with a work for the sole purpose of illustration for instruction does not infringe copyright in the work provided that the dealing is (a) for a non-commercial purpose, (b) by a person giving or receiving instruction (or preparing for giving or receiving instruction), and (c) accompanied by a sufficient acknowledgement." [legislation.gov.uk](https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/1988/48/section/32)

**Claim 7**: The UK IPO guidance expressly states that the fair dealing exception does not remove the need for photocopying licences in schools and that schools and universities still need to hold CLA licences. Where no licence exists, a teacher may copy up to 5% of a work per annum.
- **Source**: UK IPO "Exceptions to copyright: Education and Teaching" — "Does this mean schools/universities no longer need photocopying licences? No, schools and universities still need to hold photocopying licences. However, where works are not available under licence, a teacher may photocopy extracts from works without worrying about copyright infringement, as long as they copy no more than 5% of the work per annum." [UK IPO guidance PDF](https://assets.publishing.service.gov.uk/government/uploads/system/uploads/attachment_data/file/375951/Education_and_Teaching.pdf)

**Claim 8**: The CLA Education Licence covers Cambridge University Press publications. It permits schools to copy up to one chapter or 5% of a book (whichever is greater), and one article from a journal. Copies may be shared with students/staff on a secure network only.
- **Source**: CLA Schools Licence page and CLA Education Licence Terms — "Make copies from books, magazines, journals and websites ... Copy up to one article, chapter, short story or poem or 5% of the total, whichever is greater." [CLA Schools Licence](https://cla.co.uk/cla-products/schools-licence/); [CLA Education Licence Terms](https://www.cla.co.uk/education-licence-terms/); [CLA Included Publishers](https://cla.co.uk/included/he-digital/)

**Claim 9**: The UK Department for Education buys CLA licences for all state-funded primary and secondary schools in England, covering most of their copyright requirements.
- **Source**: GOV.UK guidance — "The Department for Education (DfE) buys copyright licences for all state-funded primary and secondary schools in England – covering schools for almost all their copyright requirements." [DfE guidance](https://www.gov.uk/guidance/copyright-licences-for-state-schools-in-england)

**Claim 10**: Vietnam's IP Law 2022 (Art.25) permits non-commercial use of published works without permission in certain cases including: (a) self-copying one copy for research/study (not by copying device); (b) reasonable partial copying by copying device; (c) reasonable use as illustration in lectures (including on internal networks with technical access controls); (e) library non-commercial use; and others.
- **Source**: WIPO Lex — Vietnam Law 07/2022/QH15, Art.25; confirmed in English translations by Kenfox Law, VietAn Law, and Mondaq. [WIPO Lex](https://www.wipo.int/wipolex/en/legislation/details/21740)

**Claim 11**: Decree 17/2023/ND-CP, implementing the Vietnam IP Law, defines "reasonable partial reproduction" as no more than one copy of a part of the work (Art.25), and requires that lecture illustration use be limited to the class with access restricted to teachers and students (Art.26).
- **Source**: Decree 17/2023/ND-CP Art.25, Art.26 — "reasonable reproduction of part of works by reproduction devices for personal scientific research or learning and not for commercial purposes ... means an act of reasonably reproducing no more than one copy of part of a work." [DAZPRO translation of Decree 17/2023](https://dazpro.com/decree-17-2023-vietnam-on-copyright/)

**Claim 12**: Vietnamese law provides for a compulsory licensing mechanism (Decree 17, Art.37) allowing Vietnamese organizations to reproduce lawfully distributed works for non-commercial teaching and research after 3-5 years from first publication, provided they have made unsuccessful efforts to obtain permission from the copyright holder and pay royalties determined by the Ministry of Culture.
- **Source**: Decree 17/2023/ND-CP Art.37 — "Vietnamese organizations and individuals wishing to reproduce works that have been distributed or expressed to the public legitimately for teaching and research of non-commercial nature must submit application ... together with proof indicating that the applicants have previously requested copyright holders to grant them permission ... have been rejected or failed to reach an agreement." [Full text](https://dazpro.com/decree-17-2023-vietnam-on-copyright/)

**Claim 13**: The Berne Convention, Art.9(2), establishes the three-step test for copyright exceptions: they must be (1) confined to certain special cases, (2) not conflict with a normal exploitation of the work, and (3) not unreasonably prejudice the legitimate interests of the author. Art.10(2) specifically permits use of works "by way of illustration ... for teaching" if compatible with fair practice.
- **Source**: Berne Convention (Paris Act 1971) — Art.9(2) and Art.10(2). [Cornell LII](https://www.law.cornell.edu/treaties/berne/9.html); [UN Treaty Collection](https://treaties.un.org/doc/Publication/UNTS/Volume%201161/volume-1161-I-18338-English.pdf)

**Claim 14**: Both UK and Vietnam copyright exceptions are territorial. A use permitted under Vietnamese law is not automatically permitted under UK law, and vice versa. Online courses reaching students in multiple countries must comply with the copyright laws of each country where students receive the content.
- **Source**: UK IPO guidance — "Copyright is a territorial right, and different acts are permitted in different countries. You need to ensure that you comply with the laws of the countries in which you provide online resources." [UK IPO guidance](https://assets.publishing.service.gov.uk/government/uploads/system/uploads/attachment_data/file/375951/Education_and_Teaching.pdf)

**Claim 15**: The legal status of independently created supplementary educational materials that track the structure and content of a specific textbook (e.g., answer keys, study guides) is contested and unsettled, as illustrated by the Pearson v. Chegg lawsuit (settled 2023 without a ruling) which raised derivative-work and fair use questions.
- **Source**: ARL legal analysis of Pearson v. Chegg — "Pearson alleges that Chegg's answers infringe in two different ways. First, some of Chegg's answers quote or closely paraphrase ... Second, the answers are 'based upon, and necessarily derive from' Pearson's questions." [ARL blog](https://www.arl.org/blog/pearson-v-chegg-challenges-lawfulness-of-supplementary-educational-materials/)

**Claim 16**: Cambridge University Press's copyright notices warn that it may not be able to grant permission for all material it publishes — particularly illustrations, tables, poetry, or extensive quotations that may have been reproduced from other rightsholders. Teachers must check captions/citations before assuming CUP controls the rights.
- **Source**: Cambridge Rights & Permissions — "Note also that Cambridge University Press may not be able to grant permission for the use of all the material it publishes. In particular, illustrations, tables, poetry or other extensive quotations included in Cambridge publications may have been reproduced by Cambridge with the permission of another rightsholder." [Cambridge permission page](https://www.cambridge.org/us/information/rights/permission.htm)

**Claim 17**: The Cambridge One platform terms of use prohibit reproduction, modification, extraction, adaptation, posting, transmission, copying, making available, or distribution of any content without prior written permission.
- **Source**: Cambridge One Terms — "Except where it is otherwise indicated as acceptable on the Learning Environment, you may not reproduce, modify, extract, adapt, post, transmit, copy, make available or distribute or otherwise use any of the content on this Learning Environment without our prior written permission." [Cambridge One Terms](https://www.cambridgeone.org/terms)
